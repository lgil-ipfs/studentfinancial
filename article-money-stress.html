Money Stress Is Real: How to Cope

10 min read • Updated April 2026

We need to talk about the physical feeling of checking your bank account and seeing $12. The knot in your stomach. The way your heart rate kicks up when you see an email from your student loan provider. The shame of having to decline a dinner out with friends.

Financial stress is one of the leading causes of anxiety among Canadian students. It affects your grades, your sleep, and your relationships. If you’re feeling overwhelmed, the first thing you need to know is: You are not failing. You are navigating a difficult system.

The "Head in the Sand" Trap

When money gets tight, our natural instinct is often to stop looking. We stop checking the banking app. We stop opening the envelopes. We tell ourselves "I'll deal with it when I graduate."

This is a defense mechanism, but it actually increases your background stress levels. The unknown is always scarier than the reality. The first step to coping is naming the numbers. Write them down. Once they are on paper, they are just problems to be solved, not monsters in the dark.

Immediate Relief Steps:

  • Unsubscribe: Get those "flash sale" emails out of your inbox. If you don't see the deal, you won't feel the "need" to spend.
  • Talk to a Peer: You’ll be shocked how many of your friends are in the exact same boat. Shared struggle is easier to carry.
  • The 24-Hour Rule: For any purchase over $20, wait 24 hours. The emotional "spike" of wanting it will usually fade.

Separating Your Worth from Your Net Worth

In our culture, we often confuse "being broke" with "being a failure." This is Financial Gaslighting. Your value as a human, a student, and a friend has nothing to do with the number of zeros in your savings account.

"Being a student is a temporary financial state, not a permanent character flaw."

When to Seek Professional Help

If financial anxiety is causing you to skip meals, lose sleep every night, or feel hopeless, it’s time to reach out. Most universities have emergency bursaries for students in crisis, and many non-profits (like us!) offer free, judgment-free support.
